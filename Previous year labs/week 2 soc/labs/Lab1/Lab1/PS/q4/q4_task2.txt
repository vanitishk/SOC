Task 2: Case Studies Analysis

=========================================================
Case A: Back to the Ordinary (Housing Dataset)
=========================================================
1. Baseline Comparison: 
The results do not differ significantly. The unregularized OLS model (λ = 0) performs virtually 
identically to the optimal Ridge and Lasso models. This dataset has low dimensionality (5 features) 
and sufficient samples, meaning OLS is already perfectly stable. Adding regularization here introduces 
unnecessary bias without any variance to reduce.

2. Coefficient Stability (λ = 10): 
At a high penalty of λ = 10, Ridge shrinks the coefficients by distributing small weights across all 
features, but rarely sets them to exactly zero. Lasso, which enforces sparsity, will incorrectly 
force true, relevant features to exactly zero due to the artificially high penalty, falsely identifying 
them as irrelevant.

=========================================================
Case B: The Needle in the Haystack (Gene Dataset)
=========================================================
3 & 4. Optimal Model Selection & Coefficient Inspection: 
The best Lasso model (e.g., at λ = 0.1) assigns massive weights to the few "True Genes", but retains 
tiny non-zero weights for some noise genes. To completely eliminate this remaining noise, λ would 
need to be increased further (e.g., to λ = 1.0). However, doing so would over-regularize the model, 
severely crushing the weights of the True Genes and increasing bias. The optimal model tolerates 
minor noise to protect the signal of the true features.

5. Post-Selection Inference:
(a) The Bias Problem: Lasso's penalty artificially "shrinks" the coefficients of the main genes, 
biasing their estimated impact downwards.

(b) The Two-Stage Method (Relaxed Lasso): Step 1 involves using Lasso to identify the non-zero 
features (True Genes). Step 2 involves discarding all zeroed noise features and running an 
unregularized OLS model exclusively on those identified True Genes.

(c) Execution Impact: This filters out residual noise and completely eliminates the Lasso shrinkage 
bias, yielding a better Test R^2 compared to the pure Lasso model.

6. Model Interpretability: 
Ridge regularization shrinks coefficients but does not enforce sparsity (it does not set weights to 
exactly zero). Therefore, it would output a dense vector where every gene has a non-zero weight, 
making it impossible to confidently isolate exactly the 3 target genes.

=========================================================
Case C: Imperfect Clones (Sensor Dataset)
=========================================================
1. Performance Assessment: 
The unregularized OLS baseline yields a Test MSE of ~20.56. Regularization significantly improves 
performance, with the best regularized model (Lasso at λ = 1.0) lowering the MSE to ~15.32, a vast 
mathematical improvement.

2. Cluster Analysis (Sensors 1–4): 
OLS makes the coefficients highly volatile and unstable due to collinearity (high standard deviation 
among the 4 weights). Ridge (λ = 30) successfully enforces a grouping effect, averaging the coefficients 
of the identical sensors and resulting in a very low standard deviation among them. Lasso (λ = 1.0) 
selects the sensors but fails to average them, assigning wildly different weights (e.g., 3.08, 1.29, 1.59, 2.65).

3. Noise Rejection (Sensors 9 & 10): 
Lasso completely zeroes out the pure noise sensors (9 and 10), perfectly identifying them. Ridge only 
shrinks them but fails to drop them entirely.

4. The Hybrid Pipeline:
(a) Design: Step 1 (Selection) uses Lasso to flag and drop the noise sensors (Sensors 9 and 10). 
Step 2 (Estimation) trains a Ridge regression model on the remaining 8 sensors.

(b & c) Implementation & Comparison: This hybrid strategy manually removes the noise that confuses 
Ridge, allowing Ridge to perfectly average the remaining good, correlated sensors. Consequently, 
it significantly outperforms any standalone OLS, Ridge, or Lasso model.
